CIRCUIT 1: Autodromo Nazionale Monza (Italian Grand Prix)

Track Characteristics


Location: Monza, Italy (within the Royal Park of Monza, north of Milan)
Circuit length: 5.793 km
Number of corners: 11 (6 corner complexes: two chicanes, two Lesmos, Ascari complex, Parabolica/Curva Alboreto)
Race distance: 53 laps
DRS zones: 2
Circuit type: Permanent circuit
Lap record: 1:21.046 — Rubens Barrichello (2004)
Fastest race lap in F1 history: 1:20.901 — Lando Norris (2024), average speed of 257.781 km/h


Key layout features:
Monza is the fastest circuit on the F1 calendar and has been since 1991. Cars run at full throttle for approximately 80% of the lap, placing enormous stress on engines. The circuit is defined by extremely long straights — the main start/finish straight alone measures 1,194 metres — broken by three slow chicanes added over decades for safety reasons. The Curva Grande is taken at near full throttle in seventh gear. The Lesmo 1 and Lesmo 2 are high-speed left-right corners that demand precision and full commitment from the driver. The Variante Ascari is a flowing left-right-left chicane that sets up the run to the Curva Alboreto (formerly Parabolica), the signature final corner, a long decreasing-radius right-hander onto the main straight. Maximum recorded speeds at Monza in F1 exceed 360 km/h.

Aerodynamic configuration:
Monza demands the lowest-downforce configuration of the entire season. Teams run what is known as a "Monza-spec" wing — the flattest, skinniest rear wing available — to minimise drag and maximise top speed. The sacrifice is significant cornering grip, making the car feel twitchy and nervous through the faster sections. This low-downforce compromise shifts the performance emphasis entirely to mechanical grip, traction, and braking stability.

Surface notes (post-2024 resurfacing):
The track was completely resurfaced ahead of the 2024 Italian Grand Prix. Pirelli observed that the new, darker surface offers more grip and can generate higher track temperatures in sunny conditions. Track evolution across the weekend is expected to be very high following the resurfacing, and grip levels improved significantly across the race weekend as more laps were accumulated.

Braking demands:
The braking zone into the first chicane (Variante del Rettifilo) is one of the heaviest and most demanding on the calendar. Cars decelerate from over 350 km/h and must slow to around 70–80 km/h in a matter of seconds. Braking stability and traction coming out of the two main chicanes are the primary tyre stress factors. Lateral loads in the faster corners such as the Curva Alboreto also contribute to tyre work.


Tyre Compounds

Pirelli compound selection (2023–2024): C3 (Hard), C4 (Medium), C5 (Soft)

These are the three softest compounds in Pirelli's range, which may seem counterintuitive given that Monza is a power circuit. The reasoning is that low-downforce cars generate very little lateral load and tyre energy through corners, requiring softer compounds to bring the tyres into their optimal operating temperature window.

Compound behaviour:


C5 (Soft): Primarily a qualifying tyre. Too high in degradation for race use at Monza due to heat build-up. Teams typically target stints of 14–20 laps on the Soft if used as a race starting compound.
C4 (Medium): The workhorse compound. Most drivers use the Medium for the first stint. In 2024, Pirelli observed a tendency for graining to form on the C4, particularly on the opening laps before the track rubbers in. Graining risk diminishes significantly as the weekend progresses and track evolution advances.
C3 (Hard): The preferred second compound for the race. Low degradation, excellent durability, ideal for a long final stint. Most teams save two sets of Hards for race day.


Tyre pressures:
Pirelli mandated minimum starting pressures of 24.5 psi (front) and 22.5 psi (rear) for the 2024 Italian GP.


Historical Strategy Patterns

Primary strategy: One-stop (preferred)
Monza historically favours a one-stop strategy due to the long pit lane, which creates a significant time penalty for stopping. The pit lane at Monza is one of the longer ones on the calendar, adding several seconds of lost time compared to circuits with shorter pit lanes.

Most common race strategy combinations:


Medium → Hard (pit between laps 20–26): The most recommended approach by Pirelli. Start on C4, pit at roughly the quarter-to-third distance mark, and run Hards to the flag.
Hard → Medium (pit between laps 26–32): The reversed strategy favoured by drivers starting from the back of the grid who can begin on Hards when the track is less rubbered-in, then benefit from the Medium on a fully evolved track surface.
Soft → Hard (pit between laps 14–20): A more aggressive opening option for drivers with grid penalties or strategic flexibility.
Two-stop (Medium → Hard → Hard or Medium → Hard → Medium): Plan B only, typically triggered by graining or safety car intervention. A two-stop comes with a considerable time penalty given the long pit lane and is only pursued if tyre management proves impossible on a one-stop.


Historical race data (2023):
Eight different strategies were deployed by the top-10 finishers in 2023. Verstappen (1st) and Russell (3rd) completed single stops, while Leclerc (2nd) had already made two stops before a safety car allowed all three to stop again.

Key strategic considerations:


DRS trains can form at Monza due to the slipstream effect. Groups of cars drafting each other can become difficult to separate, meaning track position and tyre advantage can be partially cancelled by a DRS train effect.
The undercut at Monza is moderately effective but partially offset by the long pit lane penalty. Teams must weigh the time lost in pit lane against the tyre advantage gained.
Overtaking is possible at Monza, particularly at the first chicane and Roggia, which reduces the premium on track position compared to Monaco or Singapore.



Weather Tendencies


Typical race conditions: The Italian Grand Prix is held in early September. Conditions are typically hot and sunny, with ambient temperatures of 25–32°C and track temperatures frequently exceeding 45–50°C, particularly post-resurfacing with the darker 2024 asphalt.
Rain probability: Rain is possible but uncommon in early September at Monza. The circuit has a moderate history of mixed conditions but is not considered a high-risk wet weather venue.
Temperature impact on strategy: High track temperatures increase the risk of graining on the C4 Medium, which can force teams toward a two-stop approach or require aggressive tyre management to maintain a one-stop.



Safety Car Probability

Rating: Moderate (approximately 31% of races since 1993)

Despite Monza's wide track and relatively open layout compared to street circuits, the safety car appears at Monza in roughly one in three races. The primary causes are:


Heavy braking incidents at the first chicane, which is a known flashpoint for first-lap contact
Mechanical failures caused by the extreme engine stress of the near-80%-throttle lap
DRS-related contact at high speeds on the straights


Strategic impact of safety car:
A safety car at Monza significantly disrupts one-stop plans, typically triggering a second stop for all drivers to take advantage of pit lane closure time recovery. Teams holding back a second set of Hards are positioned to react effectively.


Undercut / Overcut Effectiveness


Undercut: Moderately effective but partially negated by the long pit lane. The time loss in pit lane reduces the net gain from pitting early and attacking on fresh tyres. A successful undercut typically requires the car ahead to be on noticeably older rubber.
Overcut: Works well at Monza when the track is evolving rapidly, particularly early in the weekend. Staying out on old tyres while a rival pits can gain track position, especially if the driver can manage degradation.
General note: Overtaking is possible at Monza due to the long straights and DRS zones, which slightly reduces the strategic premium placed on the undercut compared to circuits where on-track passing is nearly impossible.




CIRCUIT 2: Yas Marina Circuit (Abu Dhabi Grand Prix)

Track Characteristics


Location: Yas Island, Abu Dhabi, United Arab Emirates
Circuit length: 5.281 km
Number of corners: 16 (reduced from 21 following the 2021 reconfiguration)
Race distance: 58 laps
DRS zones: 2
Circuit type: Permanent circuit
Direction: Counter-clockwise
Lap record: 1:25.637 — Kevin Magnussen (2024)
Longest straight: 1.2 km (between Turn 5 and Turn 6/7), cars reach 320+ km/h


Key layout features:
Yas Marina was designed by Hermann Tilke and opened in 2009. The circuit underwent a major reconfiguration in 2021, reducing corner count from 21 to 16 and significantly shortening lap times by approximately 13 seconds. The changes opened up three key sections: the North Hairpin area (previous chicane removed), the South Marina (sweeping banked curve replacing a series of slow corners), and the Hotel section (less tight to allow higher cornering speeds). The 2021 redesign created a faster, more flowing circuit with additional overtaking opportunities.

The circuit is famous for passing beneath the iconic W Abu Dhabi Hotel, whose LED facade lights up the night sky. The race takes place in a twilight/night format, transitioning from daylight to full artificial illumination as the race progresses.

The pit lane at Yas Marina is unique in Formula 1: the pit exit features a tunnel that passes underneath the track before rejoining the circuit on the outside of Turn 3. Total pit lane length is approximately 1.07 km — one of the longer pit lanes on the calendar, increasing the time cost of each stop.

Surface notes:
The track surface is made of Graywacke aggregate, the same material used at Bahrain. It is highly acclaimed for its consistent grip levels. The track underwent partial resurfacing from Turns 1 to 4 ahead of the 2025 race, smoothing bumps and sharpening load transitions. The surface provides predictable thermal degradation behaviour, primarily at the rear axle.

Three-sector structure:


Sector 1: Two quick sections before a heavy braking zone — the primary overtaking spot
Sector 2: Two high-speed straights containing both DRS zones, each ending in heavy braking zones
Sector 3: A complex of lower-speed corners through the Hotel section, placing a premium on aerodynamic balance and rear grip



Tyre Compounds

Pirelli compound selection (consistent across recent seasons): C3 (Hard), C4 (Medium), C5 (Soft)

Pirelli consistently selects the three softest compounds for Abu Dhabi. The track places medium-low forces on the tyres and is considered to be on the lower end of tyre demand, despite vertical loads on the front axle being notable.

Compound behaviour:


C5 (Soft): Used almost exclusively in qualifying. The Soft shows relatively high degradation in race conditions and Pirelli does not typically expect it to play a significant role in race strategy.
C4 (Medium): The primary race starting compound. Most drivers start on the Medium and run it for the opening stint. Graining is possible, particularly in the cooler night conditions as temperatures drop, narrowing the tyre's operating window.
C3 (Hard): The dominant second-stint compound. Highly durable, well-suited to the second half of the race, particularly the closing stint in cooler ambient conditions. Pirelli favours the Hard for long final stints and teams typically save multiple sets.


Temperature note:
Abu Dhabi is a twilight-to-night race, which means track temperatures drop significantly as the race progresses. FP1 and FP3 take place in daylight; FP2 and the race start at sunset. This temperature transition narrows tyre operating windows later in the race and can increase graining risk.


Historical Strategy Patterns

Primary strategy: One-stop (historical default, shifting toward two-stop)
Historically, Abu Dhabi was a one-stop race. However, since the 2021 circuit reconfiguration added more overtaking opportunities, the strategic calculation has shifted. Pirelli and the teams now consider two stops as the preferable approach when tyre degradation permits.

Most common race strategy combinations:


Medium → Hard (pit between laps 12–20): The primary one-stop strategy. Starts on C4, switches to C3 around the end of the first third of the race.
Two-stop: Medium → Hard → Hard (first stop laps 12–18, second stop laps 33–39): Pirelli's recommended approach in 2024 given graining concerns. Both stints on the Hard are long, fresh rubber stints that benefit from cool night temperatures.
Hard → Medium → Hard: Less common alternative for drivers starting from unusual grid positions.


Key historical context:


The undercut is described by Pirelli as "very effective" at Abu Dhabi. The 2021 layout changes mean drivers can no longer easily defend track position, increasing the benefit of undercutting.
A one-stop requires significant tyre management to limit performance degradation; it is possible but demands discipline throughout.
Pirelli stated in 2024: "On a track where graining could have a significant impact and where overtaking is possible, a two-stop strategy certainly seems preferable."


Pit stop window (two-stop):


First stop: Laps 12–18
Second stop: Laps 33–39



Weather Tendencies


Race format: Twilight/night race. Starts at sunset (approximately 17:00 local time) and concludes in full darkness.
Typical conditions: Dry. Abu Dhabi has extremely low rainfall probability during the December race weekend. Rain in Abu Dhabi in December is an extreme rarity.
Temperature profile: Ambient temperatures begin in the 25–28°C range at the start and drop to 20–23°C by the end of the race. Track temperatures follow a more dramatic curve, dropping from 35°C+ during daylight practice to significantly cooler levels under the night sky.
Strategic implication: The temperature drop throughout the race creates a changing tyre environment. Compounds that work in early stints may behave differently in cooler conditions later in the race, affecting degradation rates.



Safety Car Probability

Rating: Low to Moderate

The 2021 circuit changes improved overtaking and reduced the amount of defensive driving that previously caused frustration, but they did not significantly increase incident probability. Abu Dhabi is a permanent circuit with ample run-off, and incident rates are relatively low.

Historical note: The most famous safety car deployment in Abu Dhabi was the 2021 season finale, which directly determined the Drivers' Championship outcome and triggered significant controversy around race management.

Strategic implication of safety car:
A well-timed safety car or Virtual Safety Car (VSC) can turn a marginal one-stop plan into a comfortable two-stop by allowing a free pit stop under reduced time cost. Teams holding a set of Hards in reserve are well-positioned to react.


Undercut / Overcut Effectiveness


Undercut: Very effective at Abu Dhabi, per Pirelli. The 2021 layout changes opened the circuit and reduced the ability to defend track position on older tyres. A driver with fresher tyres can close the gap rapidly on the faster post-2021 layout.
Overcut: Less reliable than the undercut. The high risk of graining on extended stints makes staying out for a long time a gamble, and the improved overtaking opportunities mean falling behind on track after a late stop is not impossible to recover from, but it is risky.
General note: The effective undercut at Abu Dhabi reinforces the preference for two stops — the first stop allows teams to take track position aggressively, and the second refreshes tyres for the cooler final portion of the race.




CIRCUIT 3: Baku City Circuit (Azerbaijan Grand Prix)

Track Characteristics


Location: Baku, Azerbaijan (runs through the city centre along the Caspian Sea waterfront)
Circuit length: 6.003 km
Number of corners: 20
Race distance: 51 laps
DRS zones: 2
Circuit type: Temporary street circuit
Direction: Counter-clockwise
Lap record: 1:43.009 — Charles Leclerc, Ferrari (2019)
Main straight length: 2.2 km along Neftchilar Avenue (one of the longest flat-out sections in Formula 1)
Top speed: Cars regularly exceed 350 km/h and have recorded up to 360 km/h on the main straight
Narrowest section: 7.6 metres through the Old City/Castle section (Sector 2)


Key layout features:
The Baku City Circuit is the second-longest track on the current F1 calendar (after Jeddah) and the fastest street circuit in the world. Designed by Hermann Tilke, the circuit is defined by extreme contrasts between its three sectors. It runs anti-clockwise through the historic centre of Baku and along the shores of the Caspian Sea.

Three-sector breakdown:


Sector 1 (Government Quarter): A series of predominantly 90-degree corners running through the modern government district. Right-angle turns place heavy demands on brakes and tyres through repeated aggressive deceleration. Entry to Turn 1 is a heavy braking zone from over 350 km/h. Tyre punctures occur with above-average frequency in Sector 1 due to the aggressive lock-up risk.
Sector 2 (Old City / Castle Section): The most technically demanding and dangerous section of the lap. The track narrows to 7.6 metres — barely wider than a Formula 1 car — as it threads through the walls of Baku's UNESCO World Heritage Old City. Three corners pass in roughly one second and any miscalculation in the entry angle results in immediate contact with the barriers. This section has claimed multiple high-profile qualifying crashes, including Leclerc's self-described "I am stupid" incident in 2019 (Turn 8/9 wall contact). Zero margin for error. Qualifying red flags in this sector are common.
Sector 3 (Castle to Finish): Opens up dramatically after the confinement of the Old City. The track widens and transitions to a 2.2 km flat-out blast along the Caspian Sea waterfront (Neftchilar Avenue) back to the start/finish line. This is one of the longest sustained full-throttle sections in Formula 1 and is where the slipstream effect is most powerful. Two DRS zones accelerate the drafting advantage further.


Aerodynamic configuration:
Baku demands a compromise setup unlike almost any other circuit. The Castle section requires sufficient downforce for mechanical grip and stability through the tight corners, while the 2.2 km straight requires minimal drag for top speed. Teams typically run a medium-to-low downforce configuration, accepting a deficit in aerodynamic grip through Sector 2 to prioritise straight-line performance.

Wind factor:
Baku is a coastal city exposed to significant winds from the Caspian Sea. The city's Azerbaijani name (Bākuh) translates as "city of winds" or "a place where the breeze is severe." Wind affects car balance and downforce generation significantly, particularly on the long straight where lateral gusts can destabilise an already lightly-loaded car. Wind conditions are unpredictable and can change direction and strength session to session.

Surface and track evolution:
As a street circuit normally open to city traffic, the Baku surface is significantly less grippy than a permanent racing circuit at the start of the weekend. Track evolution is very high throughout Friday and Saturday as rubber is laid down. Grip levels are known to vary from section to section of the circuit. Pirelli engineers noted in 2024 that grip levels were lower than in previous seasons and differed significantly between sectors.


Tyre Compounds

Pirelli compound selection (2024): C3 (Hard), C4 (Medium), C5 (Soft)

Pirelli compound selection (2025): C4 (Hard), C5 (Medium), C6 (Soft) — one step softer than 2024

Pirelli consistently selects its softest compounds for Baku, consistent with its approach to all street circuits where low grip levels and abrasiveness require compounds that can operate at the lower end of their temperature range. In 2025, Pirelli moved one compound softer specifically to open up the possibility of a two-stop strategy — the 2024 compound selection with the existing car's tyre management capability would have predictably produced a near-universal one-stop race.

Compound behaviour:


Soft (C5 in 2024, C6 in 2025): Qualifying compound. The Soft is exposed to significant rear overheating risk during acceleration out of Baku's 90-degree corners, where traction demands are extreme. Not generally used in race strategy.
Medium (C4 in 2024, C5 in 2025): The most popular race starting compound. The majority of drivers (14 of 20 in 2024) started on the Medium. Graining can develop depending on the track's state of rubber evolution.
Hard (C3 in 2024, C4 in 2025): The dominant race compound for the long stint. Low degradation, high durability. Teams save multiple sets of Hards. In 2024, Pierre Gasly completed a 50-lap stint on the Hard before switching to Softs on the penultimate lap — the longest documented stint in the 2024 Baku race.


Rear tyre overheating:
A specific technical challenge at Baku is the risk of overheating the rear tyres during hard acceleration out of the many 90-degree corners in Sector 1 and the Castle section. The repeated hard traction zones build significant heat in the rear axle, requiring drivers and engineers to manage rear temperature carefully throughout stints.


Historical Strategy Patterns

Primary strategy: One-stop (strongly dominant)
Baku is a one-stop race in almost all scenarios. The combination of a long lap, low tyre wear from the street surface's relatively low abrasiveness, and the difficulty of overtaking through the technical sections means teams prioritise track position and minimise pit stops.

Most common race strategy combinations:


Medium → Hard (pit between laps 13–20): The default and optimal strategy. Start on C4, switch to C3 during the pit window and extend to the finish.
Hard → Hard or Hard → Medium: Minority option for cars starting from the back or seeking strategic differentiation.
Two-stop (Medium → Hard → Hard or similar): Only becomes a realistic option if a safety car appears in the second half of the race, enabling a near-free second stop. Pirelli noted that a two-stop "only becomes credible if there is a safety car in the second half of the race."


2024 race data:


14 of 20 drivers started on the Medium compound
Remaining 6 drivers started on the Hard
Pre-race, the entire field appeared committed to a one-stop plan
Two drivers made second stops: Stroll (puncture) and Verstappen (chasing fastest lap point)
Gasly's 50-lap Hard stint was the most extreme example of tyre management seen in the race


Two-stop pit windows (if applicable):


First stop: Laps 7–15 (short Medium opening stint)
Second stop: Laps 24–32 (second set of Hards)



Weather Tendencies


Race timing: The Azerbaijan GP moved to September from its previous April slot. September in Baku delivers notably higher temperatures than April.

Typical conditions: Hot and dry. Ambient temperatures of 25–32°C, track temperatures of 35–48°C. Sunshine is typical.
Wind: Highly variable and a genuine performance factor. Baku's coastal position means wind can affect qualifying lap times and race car balance in ways not seen at inland circuits. The 2025 qualifying session was described as the "most chaotic in Baku history" due to combined wind and wet conditions.
Rain probability: Low in September, but possible. Street circuit drainage and changing grip levels make any wet conditions significantly more unpredictable than at permanent circuits.
Strategic implication of heat: High track temperatures accelerate rear tyre degradation and increase overheating risk through Sector 1 acceleration zones.



Safety Car Probability

Rating: Very High

Baku has one of the highest safety car probabilities of any circuit on the F1 calendar. In the nine races held between 2016 and 2024, there have been seven safety car deployments, two red flags, and three Virtual Safety Cars. This amounts to a safety car or race interruption event in the vast majority of Baku races.

Primary causes:


Contact at Turn 1 (heavy braking from 350+ km/h, first-lap incidents common)
Barrier contact through the Castle section (zero run-off, any mistake ends the race)
Wall strikes on the main straight (punctures, debris, tyre failures)
Mechanical failures at high speed on the long straight (tyre delaminations, wing failures)
2021: Verstappen's tyre failure at high speed on the straight was one of the most dramatic incidents in recent F1 history


Strategic implication:
The very high safety car probability at Baku is a core element of race strategy planning. Teams must:


Maintain flexibility in their strategy to react to a safety car at any point in the race
Hold back a full set of Hards as a "free stop" reserve for when a safety car inevitably appears
Be prepared for race stoppages and red flag restarts, which can reset strategies entirely
Prioritise track position on the restart, as overtaking in the Castle section is virtually impossible


Qualifying red flags:
The Castle section is also responsible for frequent qualifying red flags. Multiple sessions have seen Q1, Q2, or Q3 red-flagged due to incidents in the Sector 2 wall complex. Teams must account for the risk of a driver failing to complete a qualifying lap.


Undercut / Overcut Effectiveness


Undercut: Moderately effective. On paper, the long straight provides overtaking opportunities, but the Castle section makes on-track passing very difficult at any circuit position other than Turn 1. An undercut that produces a gap of more than approximately 2–3 seconds makes the pass at Turn 1 straightforward, but a smaller gap requires the attacking car to make the move in a section where defending is easier.
Overcut: Rarely chosen deliberately. The high safety car probability means extending a stint carries the risk of being caught out by a safety car that benefits a rival who has already pitted.
Key strategic nuance: At Baku, timing a stop to coincide with a safety car is strategically more impactful than engineering an undercut or overcut under green-flag conditions. The most successful teams at Baku historically are those who react fastest to safety car situations, taking the free stop and exiting pit lane ahead of rivals still on track.
